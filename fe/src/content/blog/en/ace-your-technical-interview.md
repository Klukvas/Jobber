# How to Ace Your Technical Interview

Technical interviews can feel intimidating, but with the right preparation they become a structured conversation about problem-solving. Whether you're interviewing at a startup or a FAANG company, these strategies will help you perform at your best.

## 1. Understand the Format

Every company runs technical interviews differently. Before you walk in:

- **Research the process** — check Glassdoor, Blind, or the company's careers page
- **Know the rounds** — phone screen, take-home, live coding, system design, or behavioral
- **Ask the recruiter** — they want you to succeed and will often share the format upfront

> "The best candidates aren't the smartest — they're the most prepared."

## 2. Master Data Structures & Algorithms

Most coding interviews test foundational CS concepts. Focus on:

- **Arrays and strings** — sliding window, two pointers, prefix sums
- **Trees and graphs** — BFS, DFS, traversals
- **Dynamic programming** — start with simple problems and build up
- **Hash maps** — the most versatile tool in your toolkit

Practice on platforms like LeetCode or HackerRank. Aim for 2-3 problems per day over several weeks rather than cramming the night before.

## 3. Practice Coding Out Loud

In a live interview, you need to explain your thought process as you code. This feels unnatural at first:

- **Narrate your approach** before writing any code
- **State your assumptions** — input size, edge cases, constraints
- **Walk through examples** — trace your solution on a sample input
- **Discuss trade-offs** — time vs. space complexity

Practice with a friend or use mock interview platforms. Recording yourself solving problems can reveal habits you didn't know you had.

## 4. Ask Clarifying Questions

Jumping straight into coding is a red flag for interviewers. Instead:

- **Restate the problem** in your own words
- **Ask about edge cases** — empty inputs, negative numbers, duplicates
- **Clarify requirements** — sorted output? in-place modification? return type?
- **Confirm constraints** — input size helps you choose the right algorithm

Good questions demonstrate that you think before you code — exactly what employers want on the job.

## 5. Follow Up After the Interview

The interview doesn't end when you close the laptop:

- **Send a thank-you email** within 24 hours to each interviewer
- **Reflect on what went well** and what you'd improve
- **Track your progress** — note which topics came up and where you struggled
- **Keep practicing** — even if you get an offer, the skills compound
