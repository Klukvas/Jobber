# How to Write a Cover Letter That Gets You Hired

Many job seekers treat cover letters as an afterthought. That's a mistake. A strong cover letter can push you from the "maybe" pile to the top of the list - especially for roles where the hiring manager reads every application personally.

## 1. Why Cover Letters Still Matter

With so many applications automated and filtered by ATS, you might wonder if cover letters are even read. The truth: for many roles - especially at startups, small companies, and senior positions - the cover letter is the deciding factor.

A cover letter lets you:

- Explain a career gap or pivot
- Show genuine enthusiasm for the company
- Connect your experience directly to the role's needs
- Let your personality come through beyond bullet points

Even if only 30% of hiring managers read them, those are often the ones making the final call.

## 2. The Classic Cover Letter Structure

Stick to a proven structure. Don't reinvent the wheel:

**Opening paragraph** - Hook the reader. Mention the specific role and why you want *this* job at *this* company. One sentence of genuine excitement goes a long way.

**Middle paragraph(s)** - Connect your experience to the requirements. Pick 2–3 accomplishments that directly answer the job description. Be specific with numbers when you have them.

**Closing paragraph** - Reiterate your enthusiasm, thank them for their time, and include a clear call to action ("I'd love to discuss this further…").

Keep it to one page - 3–4 short paragraphs is ideal.

## 3. Tailoring Your Letter to the Job

A generic letter is worse than no letter. Hiring managers can spot a template in seconds.

To tailor effectively:

- Reference the company by name and mention something specific about their product, mission, or recent news
- Mirror the language from the job description
- Address the specific challenges the role is solving
- Adjust the tone - a startup cover letter reads differently than one for a Fortune 500

Read the job description at least twice before writing a single word.

## 4. Tone and Length

**Tone**: Professional but human. Avoid stiff corporate language ("I am writing to express my interest in…") and also avoid being overly casual. Write like you'd speak in a first interview.

**Length**: One page, 250–400 words. Shorter is usually better. Hiring managers are busy - respect their time.

**Common mistakes to avoid**:

- Repeating your resume word for word
- Starting every sentence with "I"
- Vague claims ("I'm a fast learner, team player…")
- Spelling the company name wrong

## 5. Using AI Tools to Write Cover Letters

AI can dramatically speed up cover letter writing - but only if used correctly.

**How to use AI well**:

- Feed it the job description and your resume bullet points
- Ask it to highlight the most relevant experiences
- Use it to generate a first draft, then personalize heavily
- Ask it to check your tone and tighten sentences
